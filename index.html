COPD Decision Aid Whee

A tool for education and reflection for patients with Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease who are considering their treatment options.

How do I use this website?

To go between pages on this website, use the arrow keys on your keyboard. The right arrow key will move you forward through the pages of the website, and the left arrow key will move you back (to the previous page you viewed). On any page, you can press the "H" key to hear the text on the page read aloud. On some pages, there will be more instructions on how to use the keys on the keyboard to use the website.

Who is this website meant for?

This website and the text, pictures, and videos on it are meant to inform patients with severe Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) (What is COPD?) about their treatment options. It focuses especially on the choice between managing COPD using medicines and getting a lung transplant.

What is COPD?

How do COPD treatments work?

Medicines for COPD reduce lung inflammation, making it easier to breathe. Exercising helps your lungs work better, like they did before you had COPD.

Why do you have a choice about your treatment for COPD?

Many patients with COPD wonder why they have a choice about their treatment for COPD. "Shouldn't the doctor be telling me what to do?" is a common thought.

The reason that you have been given a choice between treating COPD medically and getting a lung transplant is that neither treatment option has more of a survival benefit. In other words, most patients will live about the same amount of time no matter whether they treat their COPD with medicine or get a transplant.

Rejection

Rejection occurs when your body doesn't recognize the new lungs and treats them like an invader, attacking them like they would a virus or bacteria. This problem is partially controlled by the drugs you take, but cannot always be avoided.

Out of 100 patients like you, 30 will experience some form of rejection in the first year after transplant.
